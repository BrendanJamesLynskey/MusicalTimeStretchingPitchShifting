Musical Time-Stretching & Pitch-Shifting

An Interactive Exploration of Audio Manipulation

From magnetic tape splicing in the 1940s to neural network vocoders today, the ability to independently control the pitch and duration of sound has transformed music production, broadcasting, and creative expression. This presentation explores the history, mathematics, algorithms, and future of these fundamental audio processing techniques.

Chapter I
A History of Bending Sound

The journey from mechanical speed changes to AI-driven audio synthesis spans nearly a century of innovation. What began as crude tape manipulation evolved through digital signal processing into today's near-transparent transformations.

1940s–1950s
The Tape Era: Varispeed

Early reel-to-reel tape machines offered the simplest form of pitch manipulation: changing playback speed. Recording at a slow speed and playing back faster raised both pitch and tempo together. Les Paul was a pioneer, using tape recorders to punch in, double-track, and change pitch. Ross Bagdasarian exploited this to create "The Chipmunks" in 1958 — recording vocals at half-speed and playing back at double speed for the iconic squeaky voices.

1960s
Beatles & Creative Varispeed

The Beatles and their engineer Geoff Emerick elevated varispeed into an art form. George Martin's piano solo on "In My Life" (1965) was recorded at half-speed and played back at normal speed, creating an impossibly fast baroque performance. "Strawberry Fields Forever" (1967) famously spliced two takes recorded at different tempos and pitches — Emerick matched them by adjusting the speed of the slower take. "Rain" was recorded at a faster speed then slowed down for mixing, giving Lennon's voice a distinctive lethargic quality.

1966
The Phase Vocoder Is Born

J.L. Flanagan and R.M. Golden at Bell Laboratories published the foundational paper on the phase vocoder — an algorithm that uses the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) to analyse and resynthesize audio in the frequency domain. Originally conceived for speech compression, the phase vocoder would become the most important mathematical framework for independent time and pitch manipulation. Also in the 1960s, rotary tape-head devices like the Eltro "Information Rate Changer" (famously used for HAL 9000's voice in 2001: A Space Odyssey) offered early mechanical time-stretching.

1975
The Eventide H910 Harmonizer

Designed by Tony Agnello at Eventide, the H910 was the world's first commercially available digital pitch-shifter. Using a dual-tap delay architecture with triangle-wave crossfading, it could shift pitch in real-time. TV stations used it to correct the chipmunk voices caused by speeding up reruns for extra ad time. Musicians embraced both its clean shifting and its distinctive glitchy artifacts — some lamented when the improved H949 (1977) removed those glitches. Jon Anderson of Yes was the first artist to use the Harmonizer on his album Olias of Sunhillow (1976).

1976–1986
Digital Phase Vocoder Implementations

Michael Portnoff (1976) showed how the phase vocoder could be implemented digitally using the FFT, opening the door to practical software implementations. Mark Dolson's influential 1986 tutorial brought the technique to the computer music community. Meanwhile, the French company Publison released the "Infernal Machine 90" — a hybrid digital signal processor capable of pitch shifting, delay, reverb, and sample manipulation.

1988–1990
PSOLA: A Time-Domain Revolution

Charpentier and Stella (1988) introduced Pitch-Synchronous Overlap and Add (PSOLA), and Moulines and Charpentier refined TD-PSOLA (1990). Unlike frequency-domain methods, TD-PSOLA works directly on the waveform by identifying pitch periods and rearranging them. This approach naturally preserves formants (the resonance characteristics of a voice), avoiding the "Chipmunk effect" that plagues simple resampling.

1997–1999
Auto-Tune & Phase Vocoder Breakthroughs

Andy Hildebrand's Auto-Tune (1997) revolutionised pitch correction for vocals. In 1999, Laroche and Dolson published their landmark improvement to the phase vocoder, introducing phase-locking to maintain vertical coherence between frequency bins. This dramatically improved the quality of time-stretching, resolving the "phasiness" that had plagued earlier implementations for over three decades.

2001–2010s
DAW Integration & Commercial Algorithms

Ableton Live (2001) and other DAWs integrated time-stretching and pitch-shifting as core features. Companies like zplane developed commercial algorithms (élastique) used behind the scenes in most major DAWs. These combined multiple techniques — separating signals into sinusoidal and transient components, or using wavelet transforms — to achieve high-quality results across all types of audio material.

2020–Present
Neural Audio Synthesis & DDSP

Google Magenta's DDSP (Differentiable Digital Signal Processing) combined neural networks with classical DSP modules, enabling high-quality timbre transfer with minimal training data. The DDSP-VST plugin (2022) brought this to DAWs. More recent work includes RAVE (Caillon & Esling, 2021) using variational autoencoders, and diffusion-based models like AFTER for near-lossless audio transformation. These approaches promise to make the distinction between "time-stretching" and "resynthesis" increasingly blurred.

Chapter II
The Fundamental Problem

In the physical world, pitch and duration are inextricably linked. A guitar string vibrating faster produces both a higher pitch and shorter period. A record spinning faster sounds higher and plays quicker. The central challenge of time-stretching and pitch-shifting is to decouple these two properties that nature has bound together.

Time-stretching changes duration without affecting pitch. Pitch-shifting changes pitch without affecting duration. These are related operations: pitch-shifting is typically achieved by time-stretching followed by resampling (or vice versa).

To pitch-shift up by a factor α: first time-stretch the signal to be α times longer, then resample at α times the rate. The signal returns to its original duration but all frequencies are multiplied by α. To shift down, stretch shorter and resample slower.

Chapter III
Implementation Approaches

Algorithms for time-stretching and pitch-shifting fall into four broad families: time-domain methods that manipulate the waveform directly, frequency-domain methods built on the STFT, hybrid approaches that combine both, and modern neural methods.

Time Domain

OLA — Overlap-Add

The simplest approach. The signal is split into overlapping frames, which are repositioned (closer together or further apart) and crossfaded. Spacing frames further apart stretches the signal; closer together compresses it. Simple but produces phase discontinuities and "flanging" artifacts with polyphonic material.

Time Domain

SOLA — Synchronised OLA

Improves on OLA by using cross-correlation to find the optimal overlap position for each frame, minimising phase discontinuities. SOLA calculates the best alignment point within a search region, producing much cleaner results. Typical processing latency is around 100 milliseconds.

Time Domain

TD-PSOLA

Pitch-Synchronous Overlap-Add uses pitch detection to identify individual pitch periods in the waveform, then rearranges windows centred on these periods. Shifting windows closer raises pitch; further apart lowers it. Naturally preserves formants, avoiding the "chipmunk effect." Excellent for monophonic voice, but requires accurate pitch detection and struggles with polyphonic material.

Frequency Domain

Phase Vocoder

The workhorse of audio time-stretching. Uses the STFT to decompose audio into overlapping spectral frames. Time-stretching is achieved by changing the hop size between analysis and synthesis frames while adjusting phases to maintain horizontal coherence. Laroche & Dolson's 1999 phase-locking improvement was a watershed moment for quality. Works on polyphonic material but can produce "phasiness" and smear transients.

Frequency Domain

Sinusoidal Modelling

Identifies spectral peaks across STFT frames and connects them into sinusoidal "tracks." These tracks can be independently resynthesized at any time scale. More computationally expensive but yields excellent results on both polyphonic and percussive material, especially when the signal is separated into sub-bands.

Frequency Domain

Constant-Q Transform

Uses a frequency-domain representation with logarithmically-spaced bins matching musical pitch perception. Unlike the linear spacing of the FFT, this aligns directly with musical semitones, making pitch manipulation more natural. However, perfect reconstruction is more complex than with the standard STFT.

Hybrid

Transient + Sinusoidal Separation

High-end commercial algorithms (like zplane's élastique) separate the signal into transient and sinusoidal components. Transients are handled in the time domain to preserve their sharp attack character, while sustained sinusoidal content is processed via the phase vocoder. This avoids the smeared transients that plague pure frequency-domain methods.

Hybrid

Adaptive Wavelet Methods

Zynaptiq's ZTX technology uses adaptive wavelet transforms that provide high localisation in both time and frequency domains simultaneously. This overcomes the fundamental time-frequency resolution trade-off inherent to fixed-window STFT methods (the Heisenberg/Gabor uncertainty principle for signals).

Hybrid

PVSOLA

Phase Vocoder with Synchronised Overlap-Add combines phase vocoder spectral processing with SOLA's waveform-level synchronisation. The phase vocoder handles sinusoidal components while SOLA-style overlap matching ensures proper alignment, producing improved results for polyphonic material.

Neural / AI

DDSP — Differentiable DSP

Google Magenta's approach uses a neural autoencoder where the decoder controls classical DSP modules (harmonic synthesizer, noise filter, reverb) rather than generating waveforms directly. An RNN extracts f₀ and loudness contours, and the decoder predicts synthesis parameters. Requires only minutes of training data but is limited to monophonic signals. The DDSP-VST plugin enabled real-time use in DAWs.

Neural / AI

RAVE — Realtime Audio Variational autoEncoder

Replaces RNNs with causal CNNs, enabling faster training and 48kHz synthesis with improved timbre modelling. The variational autoencoder with discriminator training produces higher fidelity with similar data efficiency to DDSP. Supports real-time performance with low-latency inference.

Neural / AI

CLPCNet & Diffusion Models

Controllable LPCNet extends neural vocoders for explicit pitch-shifting and time-stretching control. Diffusion-based models like AFTER combine diffusion principles with neural audio coding for near-lossless timbre transfer, though at higher computational cost. These represent the cutting edge of neural audio manipulation.

Chapter IV
The Mathematics in Detail

The mathematical framework underpinning modern time-stretching and pitch-shifting centres on the Short-Time Fourier Transform, phase propagation, and overlap-add reconstruction. Here we examine the key equations in depth.

1 · The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) ▼
X(m, ω) = Σₙ x[n] · w[n − mRₐ] · e−jωn
Windowed DFT computed at successive analysis positions mRₐ
X(m, ω)Complex spectral coefficient at time frame m and frequency ω
x[n]Input discrete-time audio signal
w[n]Analysis window function (e.g. Hann window), typically N samples long
mFrame index (m = 0, 1, 2, ...)
RₐAnalysis hop size — the number of samples between consecutive analysis frames
nSample index within the signal
ωAngular frequency variable (ω = 2πk/N for bin index k)
e−jωnComplex exponential basis function of the Fourier Transform
jImaginary unit (j² = −1)
NFFT size (number of frequency bins, typically 1024–8192)
2 · Amplitude–Phase Decomposition ▼
X(m, ω) = |X(m, ω)| · ejφ(m, ω)
where   |X| = √(Re² + Im²)  and  φ = atan2(Im, Re)
|X(m,ω)|Magnitude (amplitude) spectrum — how loud each frequency is
φ(m, ω)Phase spectrum — the position within the cycle of each sinusoidal component
Re, ImReal and imaginary parts of the complex STFT coefficient
atan2Four-quadrant arctangent, giving phase in range (−π, π]
3 · Phase Vocoder: Time-Stretch Phase Propagation ▼
Δφ(m, ω) = φ(m, ω) − φ(m−1, ω) − ωRₐ
Δφ_wrapped = Δφ − 2π · round(Δφ / 2π)
ω_true = ω + Δφ_wrapped / Rₐ
φₛ(m, ω) = φₛ(m−1, ω) + ω_true · Rₛ
Core phase propagation equations of the phase vocoder
Δφ(m,ω)Phase difference between consecutive frames at frequency ω, minus the expected phase advance
φ(m,ω)Measured analysis phase at frame m, frequency ω
ωRₐExpected phase advance for a sinusoid at bin frequency ω over one analysis hop
Δφ_wrappedPhase deviation wrapped to the range [−π, π] — captures the true instantaneous frequency offset
round()Nearest-integer rounding function, used to unwrap the phase modulo 2π
ω_trueEstimated true instantaneous frequency of the sinusoidal component in this bin
RₐAnalysis hop size (samples between input frames)
RₛSynthesis hop size (samples between output frames). The ratio Rₛ/Rₐ = time-stretch factor
φₛ(m,ω)Synthesis phase — the adjusted phase used for output frame m. Propagated from the previous frame using the true frequency and synthesis hop.
4 · Overlap-Add Reconstruction (Inverse STFT) ▼
y[n] = Σₘ wₛ[n − mRₛ] · IFFT{ |X(m,ω)| · ejφₛ(m,ω) }
Time-domain reconstruction by windowed overlap-add of IFFT frames
y[n]Reconstructed time-domain output signal
wₛ[n]Synthesis window (often the same shape as the analysis window)
mRₛSynthesis time position for frame m (spaced by the synthesis hop)
IFFT{}Inverse Fast Fourier Transform — converts modified spectrum back to time domain
|X(m,ω)|Original magnitude spectrum (preserved from analysis)
φₛ(m,ω)Modified synthesis phase (from the phase propagation step)
ΣₘSummation over all synthesis frames — the "overlap-add" operation
5 · FFT Frequency Resolution & the Uncertainty Principle ▼
Δf = fₛ / N
Δf · Δt ≥ 1 / (4π)   (Gabor limit)
You cannot simultaneously achieve arbitrarily precise time and frequency resolution
ΔfFrequency resolution of the FFT — the spacing between adjacent frequency bins in Hz
fₛSampling rate in Hz (e.g. 44100 for CD-quality audio)
NFFT size (window length in samples). Larger N → better frequency resolution but poorer time resolution
ΔtTime resolution — determined by the window length. Δt ≈ N/fₛ seconds
1/(4π)The Gabor limit — the theoretical minimum product of time and frequency uncertainties for any signal representation
6 · TD-PSOLA Synthesis ▼
y[n] = Σₖ hₖ[n − tₖ'] · x[n − tₖ + tₖ']
Overlap-add of pitch-period-aligned windows at new synthesis marks
y[n]Output signal after pitch modification
x[n]Input signal
tₖAnalysis pitch marks — time positions of detected pitch period boundaries in the input signal
tₖ'Synthesis pitch marks — repositioned time positions in the output. Closer together → higher pitch; further apart → lower pitch
hₖ[n]Windowing function (typically Hann) centred at each synthesis mark, spanning two pitch periods
ΣₖSummation over all pitch-period windows — the overlap-add reconstruction
7 · SOLA Cross-Correlation Alignment ▼
Δ_opt = argmax_Δ Σₙ y[n] · x_m[n + Δ]
for Δ ∈ [−L/2, L/2] within the search region
Δ_optOptimal shift offset that maximises waveform similarity in the overlap region
argmax_Δ"Argument of the maximum" — finds the value of Δ that maximises the cross-correlation
y[n]Already-constructed output signal (the tail end that will be overlapped)
x_m[n]Current analysis frame to be aligned and added
LWidth of the search region in samples. Larger L → better matches but more computation

Chapter VI
Future Developments

The boundary between signal processing and machine learning is dissolving. Several emerging directions promise to fundamentally reshape how we manipulate audio in time and pitch.

🧠

End-to-End Neural Vocoders

Models like DDSP, RAVE, and emerging diffusion-based architectures are converging toward systems that can resynthesize any audio with arbitrary pitch and time modifications while preserving naturalness. Future systems may eliminate the need for separate analysis-modification-resynthesis pipelines entirely.

🎸

Polyphonic Neural Pitch Editing

Current DDSP methods are limited to monophonic signals. Research into polyphonic neural synthesis could enable per-note pitch and timing control within complex mixtures — imagine adjusting individual notes in a piano chord or the pitch of one instrument within a full mix.

⚡

Real-Time On-Device Processing

Optimised architectures like DDSP-CNN-Tiny demonstrate that neural audio synthesis can run on CPUs in real-time. Future hardware acceleration (dedicated neural DSP chips) may enable high-quality neural pitch-shifting and time-stretching in embedded devices, guitar pedals, and mobile phones.

🎤

Formant-Aware Intelligent Shifting

Rather than naively shifting all frequencies, future systems will model the full vocal tract and instrument resonance. This means transposing a soprano recording down an octave could produce a natural alto timbre, not just a slowed-down soprano. Disentangling pitch, timbre, formants, and expression is an active research frontier.

🌊

Adaptive Multi-Resolution Analysis

Variable-window and adaptive wavelet methods (pioneered at IRCAM) automatically adjust time-frequency resolution based on signal content. Future systems will intelligently switch between fine time resolution for transients and fine frequency resolution for sustained tones, overcoming the Gabor uncertainty limit in practice.

🔗

Differentiable Everything

The DDSP paradigm of making signal processing differentiable extends beyond synthesis. Future audio tools may use end-to-end differentiable pipelines for mixing, mastering, and spatial audio, where neural networks learn to control banks of classical DSP processors. The DAFx 2025 conference already showcased KAN-based filter design and differentiable biquad estimation within neural frameworks.
